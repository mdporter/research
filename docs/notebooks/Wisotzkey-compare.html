4 Other Variables

  • Plots are faceted by diagnosis of CHD or CM. Data for patients with other PRIM_DX are excluded.
  • The horizontal dotted line gives the average 1 year survival for the cohort (CM = 96.8%; CHD = 87.6%)

What to look for:

  • Variables that have a pattern for both CM and CHD patients. This provides strongest evidence that the variable is influential.

  • Variables that show a clear pattern for only one type of patient. These can still be useful for a diagnosis specific calculator.

  • Clinically relevant variables that are missing here indicate that there wasn’t a category/binning that had a statistically significant impact on outcome. But this doesn’t mean that won’t be helpful in a predictive calculator.

5 Wisotzkey Variables

These are the top variables identified in the Wisotzkey paper. You will notice that not many of these plots show important variables. This is because they are split by CHD vs. CM; most of the Wisotzkey variables were really helping to distinguish between the two types of patients. E.g., CHD patients are generally younger and lighter; only CHD patients can be Single Ventricle; most CHD patients have had surgeries prior to listing.

6.3 CHD Only

Adding in Laterality Disorder sub-category. With the help of ChatGPT (needs review!) I used these CHD conditions to define a new Laterality Disorder (CHD_LAT) sub-diagnosis:

  • Dextrocardia (CHD_DEX)
  • Situs Inversus (CHD_SI)
  • Heterotaxy (CHD_HETER)
  • Interrupted IVC (CHD_IIVC)
  • Bilateral SVC (CHD_BIVC)
  • Left SVC with no right SVC (CHD_LSVC)
  • Right Aortic Arch (CHD_RAA)
  • AV Discordance when part of atrial or ventricular situs abnormality (CHD_AVD)

The first three (Dextrocardia, Heterotaxy, and Situs Inversus) were significant in the univariate analysis.
